Sinharaja Forest Reserve

Sinharaja Forest Reserve is located in Sri Lanka's south-west lowland wet zone and has the last large stretch of primary lowland rainforest. It comprises of 6,092 ha of Forest Reserve and 2,772 ha of Proposed Forest Reserve, covering an area of 8,864 ha and ranging in altitude from 300 to 1,170 meters. A succession of slopes and valleys are crisscrossed by an intricate network of streams in this limited strip of undulating terrain. This intricate network of rivers flows into the Gin River on the property's southern boundary and the Kalu River on the property's northern boundary via the Napola Dola, Koskulana Ganga, and Kudawa Ganga.Annual rainfall has fluctuated between 3614 to 5006 mm over the previous 60 years, with the majority of precipitation falling during the south-west monsoon (May-July) and the north-east monsoon (August-October) (November- January). There are 830 indigenous species in Sri Lanka, with 217 trees and woody climbers found in the lowland wet zone. There are 139 (64%) of these species in the reserve, including 16 uncommon species. Birds have the highest level of endemism, with 19 (95%) of the 20 species reported in the property being indigenous to Sri Lanka. Endemism is also more than 50% among mammals and butterflies.The reserve is home to a number of threatened, endangered, and rare species, including the leopard (Panthera pardus), Indian elephant (Elephas maxiumus), endemic purple-faced Langur (Presbytis senex), Sri Lanka wood pigeon (Columba torringtoni), green-billed Coucal (Centropus chlororrhynchus), Sri Lanka white-headed starling (Sturnus senex), Sri Lanka blue magpie (Eurystomus orientalis irisi).

Horton Plains

Horton Plains in Sri Lanka is the country's coldest and windiest area. Montane evergreen forests, grasslands, marshy regions, and aquatic ecosystems are among the ecosystems found there. Horton Plains, at an elevation of 2,100 meters above sea level, covers 3,169 hectares of the island's highest tableland. Horton Plains was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site on July 30, 2010, due to its huge number of indigenous plant and fauna species.The Horton Plains, its environs, forests, and the adjacent Peak Wilderness make up Sri Lanka's most important catchment basin for nearly all major rivers. The plains are also notable for their scenic beauty and conservation value, as they hold the majority of the country's wet and montane zones' habitats and rare plants and animals. The most widespread region of Montane cloud forest on the island can be found on the western slopes. Horton Plains is more than just a naturalist's paradise. Because the vast biodiversity of Horton Plains is still largely untapped, it provides tremendous opportunity for educational and scientific purposes. All Sri Lankans have a responsibility to protect Horton Plains.
